# Product Marketing Positioning Framework

This skill walks you through April Dunford's positioning methodology to create clear, defensible positioning.

## When to Use This

- Positioning a new product or feature
- Repositioning an existing offering
- Entering a new market segment
- Clarifying muddy or unfocused positioning

## The Framework (Complete in Order)

### Step 1: Competitive Alternatives
**Question:** What would customers use if your solution didn't exist?

This isn't just direct competitors—it's the status quo. Could be:
- A competing product
- An internal solution
- A manual process
- "Do nothing"

**Critical: Don't position against yourself**
Common mistake: Positioning "beyond traditional [your own product category]" or against your previous product tier. This confuses customers and makes you compete with yourself rather than market alternatives.

Instead, identify what customers ACTUALLY compared you against or what they'd revert to if you disappeared.

**For B2B platforms:** Consider the full ecosystem
Customers often use multiple solutions for different aspects of the problem. Map out:
- Direct competitors (who do what you do)
- Partial alternatives (who solve pieces of the problem)
- Build vs. buy (internal solutions)
- Status quo / workarounds

**Output format:**
```
Primary alternatives:
1. [Alternative 1]
2. [Alternative 2]
3. [Alternative 3]

Why customers choose each alternative today:
- [Alternative 1]: [reason]
- [Alternative 2]: [reason]

Coexistence patterns (if applicable):
- Customers might use [Alternative X] for [aspect] while using us for [aspect]
- This is common when [situation]
```

### Step 2: Unique Capabilities
**Question:** What can you do that alternatives cannot?

Focus on **capabilities**, not benefits yet. Be specific and honest.

**For platforms/infrastructure:** Consider different types of differentiation
- Delivery model (managed service vs. self-serve vs. hybrid)
- Transparency and control (black box vs. full visibility)
- Technical approach (methodology, quality mechanisms, scale)
- Operational flexibility (contracting, integration, customization)

**Red flags you're going too broad:**
- Your capabilities list includes 5+ items
- Capabilities span wildly different areas
- You're listing features competitors also have
- Nothing on the list would surprise someone familiar with the space

**Better:** 2-3 capabilities that are genuinely unique or executed significantly better than alternatives

**Output format:**
```
Unique capabilities:
1. [Capability]: [Brief explanation - what it is and how it's different]
2. [Capability]: [Brief explanation - what it is and how it's different]
3. [Capability]: [Brief explanation - what it is and how it's different]

Proof points for each:
- [Capability 1]: [metric/example showing this is real, not aspirational]
- [Capability 2]: [metric/example showing this is real, not aspirational]

Why these matter more than feature X:
[Explain why you chose these over other capabilities you have]
```

### Step 3: Value (and Proof)
**Question:** What value do those capabilities enable for customers?

Connect each capability to customer outcomes. Include proof where possible.

**Critical check: Customer validation**
Beware of "[needs validation with clients]" or purely aspirational claims. If you're building positioning on assumptions, you're building on sand.

**Strong proof points:**
- Actual customer quotes
- Quantified outcomes from real implementations
- Win/loss analysis showing this drove decisions
- Usage data showing adoption

**Weak proof points:**
- "Customers tell us" (which customers? how many?)
- Internal metrics that don't tie to customer outcomes
- Aspirational ROI calculations
- Competitive claims you can't back up

**For technical/methodology positioning:**
When your differentiation is "how" you do things (scientific rigor, quality mechanisms, technical approach), you need to translate this to business outcomes:
- Methodology differentiation → Operational impact → Business value
- *(Example: "Automated validation" → "Fewer manual review cycles" → "40% faster time to production")*

**Output format:**
```
Value proposition:
[Capability] → [Immediate customer outcome] → [Business impact]

Proof points:
- Customer quote: "[Specific outcome]" - [Name, Company, Title]
- Metric: [X% improvement in Y, from case study/deployment]
- Win story: [Brief example of customer choosing you for this value]

Customer validation status:
✅ Validated: [What customers have explicitly confirmed]
⚠️ Assumed: [What we believe but need to validate]
❌ Aspirational: [What we're building toward]
```

### Step 4: Target Market Characteristics
**Question:** Who cares the most about this value?

Define the ideal customer profile based on who gets maximum value.

**The "AND" trap:**
If your ICP includes two very different segments (e.g., "enterprise AND SMB" or "technical users AND business users"), you're trying to be everything to everyone. This destroys positioning clarity.

**Instead:** Pick the segment that:
- Gets the MOST value from your unique capabilities
- Has the highest willingness to pay
- You can win consistently
- Represents your strategic direction

You can serve other segments, but your positioning should anchor on one primary ICP.

**Use Case-Based Segmentation (Advanced)**

Traditional segmentation uses firmographics (company size, industry, stage). But for platforms serving diverse customers, **use case-based segmentation** often works better.

**When to use use case segmentation:**
- Same company type has wildly different needs based on what they're doing
- Different buyer personas within same company based on use case
- Your product solves fundamentally different problems for different workflows
- Firmographics don't predict product fit well

**Use case segmentation framework:**

1. **Map actual use cases from customers:**
   - What jobs are they hiring your product to do?
   - Not features they use - the PROBLEMS they're solving
   - Group similar use cases into categories

2. **Create use case x persona matrix:**
   *(Replace with use cases and personas from your actual customer base)*
   ```
   Use Case 1 (e.g., "[Core workflow A]") x Persona A ([Primary buyer or team type])
   Use Case 2 (e.g., "[Core workflow B]") x Persona B ([Primary buyer or team type])
   Use Case 3 (e.g., "[Adjacent workflow]") x Persona C ([Primary buyer or team type])
   ```

3. **Choose where to focus:**
   - Which use case + persona combo has highest strategic value?
   - Where do your unique capabilities matter most?
   - Which can you win consistently?

4. **Create vertical-specific positioning:**
   - Core platform positioning (applies to all)
   - Use case-specific positioning (tailored messaging per vertical)
   - Separate GTM motions per vertical

**Example progression:**
*(Replace with your actual use cases — this is illustrative only)*
```
Before: "[Product] for [broad audience]" (too broad)
After:
- Vertical 1: "[Specific use case A]" → [Persona A] at [Company type A]
- Vertical 2: "[Specific use case B]" → [Persona B] at [Company type B]
- Vertical 3: "[Specific use case C]" → [Persona C] at [Company type C]

Each vertical has:
- Different value props
- Different competitive set
- Different buying process
- Separate positioning and messaging
```

**Use case segmentation pitfalls:**
- Creating too many verticals (start with 2-3 max)
- Verticals that aren't distinct enough (overlapping use cases)
- Forgetting core platform positioning that unifies them
- Not having enough customers in each vertical to validate

**When use case segmentation works:**
- You have 10+ customers per use case to validate patterns
- Different use cases have different competitive alternatives
- Sales process/buyer persona differs meaningfully by use case
- Product usage patterns cluster by use case

**For platform businesses:**
Consider buyer maturity and sophistication:
- Do they need white-glove service or prefer self-serve?
- Technical depth required to use your solution?
- Organizational maturity to implement?
- Budget authority and procurement complexity?

**Output format:**
```
Segmentation approach: [Firmographic / Use case-based / Hybrid]

If use case-based, list verticals:
Vertical 1: [Use case name]
- Primary persona: [Role, seniority]
- Job to be done: [What they're trying to accomplish]
- Why this matters strategically: [Revenue, differentiation, growth]

Vertical 2: [Use case name]
- Primary persona: [Role, seniority]
- Job to be done: [What they're trying to accomplish]
- Why this matters strategically: [Revenue, differentiation, growth]

Primary ICP (choose ONE vertical to anchor positioning):
- Use case: [Specific use case]
- Company type: [e.g., mid-market SaaS companies with dedicated ops teams]
- Company size: [Revenue, employees - be specific]
- Growth stage: [Series B+, public, etc.]
- Technical maturity: [What they already have in place]
- Budget profile: [$X-Y annual spend on this category]

Team/buyer characteristics:
- Team size: [Typical team composition]
- Key roles: [Who evaluates and who approves]
- Reporting structure: [Where they sit in org]
- Current solutions: [What they already use]
- Pain points: [What keeps them up at night]

Buying triggers: What makes them search for solutions NOW
- [Trigger 1: specific event or threshold]
- [Trigger 2: specific event or threshold]
- [Trigger 3: specific event or threshold]

Why this segment/vertical over others:
[Explain why you chose this over adjacent segments you could serve]

Secondary segments (who you also serve but don't anchor positioning on):
- [Segment 2]: [How they differ from primary]
- [Segment 3]: [How they differ from primary]
```

### Step 5: Market Category
**Question:** What market category makes your value obvious?

Choose a category that helps customers understand you quickly.

**The category should:**
- Make your differentiation obvious (not obscure it)
- Connect to how buyers budget and procure solutions
- Avoid positioning you as a commodity
- Be specific enough to be meaningful

**Category selection for platforms:**
Platforms often face a choice between:
- Established category (credible but commoditized)
- New category (differentiated but requires education)
- Hybrid category (bridges the two)

**Example patterns** *(illustrative — choose what fits your market):*
- "Revenue Intelligence" (not "sales analytics")
- "Data Observability" (not "data monitoring")
- "Workflow Automation" (not "task management")

**The "X for Y" framework:**
Sometimes works: "GitHub for data" "Figma for APIs"
Often fails: When the analogy confuses more than clarifies

**Test your category:**
- Does a buyer immediately understand what you do?
- Does it help or hurt against competitive alternatives?
- Does it force you into unfavorable comparisons?
- Can sales reps explain it in one sentence?

**Output format:**
```
Primary category: [e.g., "[Your chosen category name]"]

Why this category:
[How it helps customers understand value faster than alternatives]

What this avoids:
[Why you didn't choose obvious adjacent categories]

Alternative categories considered:
- [Category]: [Why rejected - too broad/narrow/commoditized/confusing]
- [Category]: [Why rejected - too broad/narrow/commoditized/confusing]

How buyers budget for this:
[What budget line item or comparison set this falls into]
```

### Step 6: Why Now (Market Context)

**Question:** What market shift makes your positioning newly urgent or relevant?

This is often missing from positioning work but critical for enterprise sales.

**Consider:**
- Technology shifts (AI capabilities, infrastructure changes)
- Regulatory changes (compliance requirements, data privacy)
- Economic factors (budget pressures, efficiency mandates)
- Competitive dynamics (consolidation, new entrants)
- Customer maturity (sophistication, scale)

**The "Why Now" should:**
- Explain why this matters more today than 2 years ago
- Create urgency beyond "this is a good solution"
- Validate the problem is getting bigger/more expensive
- Make your differentiation timely, not just timeless

**Output format:**
```
Market context - Why Now:
[2-3 sentences on what's changed in the market that makes your value proposition increasingly important]

Implications:
- For customers: [How the shift impacts them]
- For alternatives: [Why status quo is failing now]
- For us: [Why our approach is newly relevant]

Supporting evidence:
- [Industry trend/data point]
- [Customer behavior change]
- [Analyst insight or report]
```

### Step 7: Positioning Statement

Synthesize everything into a clear statement.

**Template:**
```
For [target customers - be specific, one primary segment]
Who [key pain point/need - the urgent problem]
[Product name] is a [market category]
That [key differentiated value - ONE main thing, not a list]
Unlike [competitive alternatives - what customers actually compare you to]
We [unique capabilities that drive value - the "how"]
```

**Positioning statement quality check:**
- ✅ Specific target (not "companies" or "teams")
- ✅ One clear value prop (not "fast, quality, and flexible")
- ✅ Honest about alternatives (not strawmen)
- ✅ Provable capabilities (not aspirational)
- ✅ Clear differentiation (sales rep could explain it)

**Common mistakes:**
- Positioning against yourself or previous products
- Multiple value props that compete for attention
- Target audience too broad (e.g., combining two very different segments in one statement)
- Features instead of capabilities
- Generic benefits that apply to anyone

## Final Deliverables

Once complete, create:

1. **Positioning statement** (using template above)

2. **Key messages** (3-5 core messages for different audiences)
   - For each audience: What they care about, how you help, proof points

3. **Proof points** (evidence supporting each message)
   - Customer stories
   - Quantified outcomes
   - Competitive wins
   - Third-party validation

4. **Competitive differentiation** (how you're different from each alternative)
   - Not just features - the "so what" for customers
   - When you win, when they win, how to compete

5. **Messaging hierarchy** (how it all connects)
   - Platform-level positioning (overall value)
   - Feature/capability positioning (specific value)
   - How features ladder up to platform narrative

## Platform vs. Feature Positioning

**For platforms with multiple capabilities:**

You need positioning at multiple levels:

**1. Platform-level positioning (the umbrella)**
- Overarching value proposition that unifies all use cases
- Core differentiation that applies everywhere
- Category definition
- Primary competitive alternatives at platform level

**2. Vertical/use case-specific positioning**
When you've segmented by use case (Step 4), each vertical needs tailored positioning:
- How the platform solves THIS use case specifically
- Value props that matter to THIS persona
- Competitive alternatives for THIS use case (often different per vertical)
- Proof points from THIS vertical

**3. Feature/capability positioning**
- How this specific capability delivers value
- Which vertical(s) it serves
- How it reinforces platform positioning
- When it matters vs. other features

**Critical: Everything must ladder up**
- Verticals should strengthen (not confuse) the platform narrative
- Features should strengthen the vertical narrative
- Test: "Does this make our positioning more clear or less clear?"

**Example hierarchy with use case segmentation:**
*(Replace with your actual platform, use cases, and features — this is illustrative only)*
```
Platform: "[Your platform name and category]"
├── Use Case 1: "[Use case A]" (for [Persona A])
│   ├── Value prop: [What matters most to this persona]
│   ├── Competitive set: [Alternatives this persona considers]
│   └── Features: [Capabilities that win here]
│
├── Use Case 2: "[Use case B]" (for [Persona B])
│   ├── Value prop: [What matters most to this persona]
│   ├── Competitive set: [Alternatives this persona considers]
│   └── Features: [Capabilities that win here]
│
└── Use Case 3: "[Use case C]" (for [Persona C])
    ├── Value prop: [What matters most to this persona]
    ├── Competitive set: [Alternatives this persona considers]
    └── Features: [Capabilities that win here]
```

**Positioning by vertical - key considerations:**

**When to create vertical-specific positioning:**
- Different buyer personas with different priorities
- Different competitive sets per use case
- Different sales motions or go-to-market approaches
- Product usage patterns cluster distinctly by vertical

**How verticals relate to platform:**
- **Shared foundation:** Core capabilities all verticals use
- **Vertical differentiation:** What makes us win in THIS vertical
- **Cross-sell narrative:** How customers expand across verticals

**Messaging discipline:**
- Platform messaging → What's true everywhere
- Vertical messaging → What's true for this use case
- Feature messaging → Supports vertical value props

**Example messaging hierarchy:**
*(Replace with your actual messages — this is illustrative only)*
```
Platform message: "[Core value prop that applies across all use cases]"
↓
Vertical 1 message ([Use case A]): "[How the platform solves this specific job]"
Vertical 2 message ([Use case B]): "[How the platform solves this specific job]"
↓
Feature message ([Key capability]): "[How this specific capability delivers the vertical value]"
```

**Common mistakes with vertical positioning:**
- Creating verticals before you have enough customers to validate patterns
- Verticals that aren't actually distinct (same buyer, same value prop)
- Forgetting to maintain coherent platform narrative
- Sales confusion about when to use which positioning
- Website/marketing that doesn't make verticals clear

## Discovering Use Case Segments

If you're considering use case-based segmentation but haven't mapped it yet, here's how to discover the patterns:

**1. Interview 10-15 customers across your base**
Ask open-ended questions:
- "Walk me through what you were trying to accomplish when you chose us"
- "What problem were you solving?" (not "which features do you use")
- "What did you try before us and why didn't it work?"
- "Who else was involved in the decision and what did they care about?"

**2. Look for clustering patterns**
Analyze responses for:
- Similar jobs-to-be-done across different companies
- Same buyer personas emerging across use cases
- Different competitive alternatives by use case
- Distinct value prop language by use case

**3. Map use case x persona matrix**
Create a 2x2 (or larger) grid:
- Rows: Use cases (the job to be done)
- Columns: Primary personas (the buyer/user)
- Cells: How many customers fit each combination

**4. Validate with data**
Check if use case patterns hold up:
- Product usage data (do features cluster by use case?)
- Win/loss analysis (do you win/lose for different reasons by use case?)
- Sales cycle data (different deal sizes or timelines by use case?)
- Customer health (retention/expansion patterns by use case?)

**5. Choose where to focus**
Prioritize use cases based on:
- Number of customers (need critical mass)
- Strategic value (revenue, growth, differentiation)
- Your unique advantage (where you win consistently)
- Market opportunity (TAM, growth rate)

**6. Test positioning by vertical**
For your top 2-3 use cases:
- Create vertical-specific messaging
- Test in sales conversations
- Measure if it resonates better than generic platform pitch
- Iterate based on customer feedback

**When use case patterns DON'T emerge:**
- Customers all hire you for similar jobs → stick with firmographic segmentation
- Usage patterns don't cluster meaningfully → one positioning approach is fine
- Competitive alternatives are the same across customers → no need for vertical positioning

## Tips for Success

**Be brutally honest about competitive alternatives**
- Include what customers ACTUALLY consider (even if it's "do nothing")
- Don't create strawman alternatives that make you look good
- Acknowledge where alternatives are legitimately strong

**Focus on outcomes customers care about, not features you're proud of**
- A feature metric only matters if it connects to a customer constraint
- Connect every capability to a specific customer pain point
- "So what?" is the most important question to ask about any feature claim

**Test your positioning with customers—do they agree?**
- Run the positioning by 3-5 customers who chose you
- Ask: "Does this match why you picked us?"
- If they correct you, listen - they're right
- If using verticals, test each vertical positioning separately

**Keep it simple enough for a sales rep to remember and use**
- If a rep can't explain your positioning in 30 seconds, simplify
- Test: Can someone unfamiliar with the company understand it?
- Avoid internal jargon and acronyms
- If you have verticals, reps need to know when to use which positioning

**Update as market/product evolves (at least quarterly review)**
- Competitive landscape changes
- Your capabilities expand
- Customer needs evolve
- Market categories shift
- Use case patterns become clearer over time

**For consultative B2B sales:**
- Acknowledge customers may use multiple solutions
- Position based on where you're strongest, not where you're weakest
- Focus on "when to choose us" not "why we're better at everything"

**For platforms/infrastructure:**
- Delivery model often matters more than features
- Transparency and control differentiate when products are similar
- Technical approach (methodology, quality) can be your moat
- Consider operational fit, not just technical fit

**For use case-based segmentation:**
- Start with 2-3 verticals max, not 5+
- Need 10+ customers per vertical to validate patterns
- Maintain platform narrative that unifies verticals
- Create separate sales plays and materials per vertical
- Measure effectiveness by vertical (conversion, deal size, velocity)

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**1. Positioning against yourself**
- Saying "moving beyond traditional [your previous product]"
- Comparing your new tier to your old tier instead of market alternatives
- Result: You compete with yourself and confuse customers
- Fix: Identify what customers used BEFORE you, not what they used within your product

**2. Listing features without connecting to customer value**
- *(Example: "We have [large number] of X and [integration/capability Y]")*
- So what? Why does that matter to the customer?
- Fix: Every feature should connect to a customer outcome

**3. Trying to be everything to everyone (too broad)**
- Combining two meaningfully different segments in a single positioning statement
- Listing too many value props: fast, high-quality, flexible, transparent, and affordable
- Result: No one knows what you're actually good at
- Fix: Pick ONE primary segment and ONE primary value prop

**4. Ignoring what customers actually say**
- Building positioning on what you want to be, not what customers chose you for
- Dismissing customer feedback that doesn't match your vision
- Result: Your positioning lands flat in sales conversations
- Fix: Interview customers who chose you - use their language

**5. Skipping the competitive alternatives step**
- Jumping straight to "what makes us great"
- Not considering status quo or internal solutions
- Result: You're solving a problem that doesn't match how customers think
- Fix: Start with alternatives, always

**6. Positioning based on unvalidated assumptions**
- "[needs validation with clients]" appears throughout positioning doc
- Basing value props on theoretical benefits
- Result: Sales encounters objections you didn't anticipate
- Fix: Only include claims you can prove with customer evidence

**7. Conflicting or ping-ponging value props**
- Using multiple unconnected positioning concepts interchangeably (e.g., mixing operational, technical, and strategic framings as if they're the same thing)
- Result: Customers don't know what you actually do
- Fix: Pick ONE anchor concept, use consistently

**8. Category choice that commoditizes you**
- Choosing a generic category name that puts you in an unfavourable competitive set
- Picking a category where differentiation is hard to express
- Result: You get compared on price to commodity alternatives
- Fix: Choose a category that makes your differentiation obvious

**9. Missing the "why now"**
- Positioning is timeless (could have been written 5 years ago)
- No connection to market shifts or urgency
- Result: Customers don't understand why to act now
- Fix: Include market context that creates urgency

**10. Forgetting about coexistence**
- Positioning assumes "winner takes all"
- In reality, customers use multiple vendors
- Result: Sales positions you as replacement when you should be complement
- Fix: Acknowledge where you fit in the ecosystem

**11. Use case segmentation without validation**
- Creating verticals based on product capabilities, not customer patterns
- Splitting into verticals before you have 10+ customers per vertical
- Result: Positioning for segments that don't actually exist as coherent markets
- Fix: Segment based on customer jobs-to-be-done, validate with real patterns

**12. Losing platform coherence with verticals**
- Each vertical has completely different messaging
- No clear through-line connecting the verticals
- Result: Company looks like 3 different products, no economies of scale in positioning
- Fix: Maintain strong platform narrative that verticals ladder up to
